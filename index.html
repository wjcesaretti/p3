The Languages of FVG

Three language families — Romance, Slavic, and Germanic — coexist in a space smaller than most U.S. counties combined.

Friulian (Furlan)

Rhaeto-Romance
~600,000 speakers · 50% of the region

A Rhaeto-Romance language — closer to French and Romansh than to Italian. The second-largest recognized minority language in Italy after Sardinian. Spoken across most of the provinces of Udine, Pordenone, and Gorizia with distinct Carnic, Central, Western, and Eastern dialects.

"Cemût staiso?""How are you?" (Central Friulian)

Triestine (Triestin)

Venetian · Romance
Dominant dialect of Trieste

A Venetian-based dialect that replaced the now-extinct Tergestine (a Rhaeto-Romance variety) by the 1800s. Enriched with German, Slovenian, Greek, and Serbo-Croatian loanwords from centuries as the Habsburg Empire's main port.

"Come xe?""How are you?" / "What's going on?" (Triestine)

Slovenian

South Slavic
~50,000 speakers in FVG

Spoken in the provinces of Gorizia, Trieste, and Udine. Protected by Italian law with rights to education and administration. Includes multiple dialects: Karst, Inner Carniolan, and the highly distinctive varieties of the Natisone, Torre, and Resia valleys.

"Kako se imate?""How are you?" (Slovenian)

Resian (Resiano)

Archaic Slavic
~1,000 speakers · Val Resia

So distinct from standard Slovenian that some linguists once classified it as a separate language. Isolated in the Val Resia since the early Middle Ages, it preserves archaic features lost in all neighboring Slavic varieties. UNESCO-classified as endangered.

"Kako se mate?""How are you?" (Resian)

Saurano (Zahrar Sproche)

South Bavarian · Germanic
~200 speakers · Sauris (1,300m)

A medieval South Bavarian dialect brought by Tyrolean colonists in the 1200s. The community is trilingual: Saurano + Italian + Carnic Friulian. Recently developed a written form — now used in WhatsApp messages.

"Bia geat's?""How's it going?" (Saurano)

Carinthian German

Bavarian · Germanic
~5,000 speakers · Val Canale

Spoken in Tarvisio and Pontebba near the Austrian border. Unlike the isolated "language islands" of the Carnic Alps, this is a continuation of the Carinthian dialect area across the border. Legacy of centuries under Habsburg rule.

"Wia geht's?""How's it going?" (Carinthian)

Bisiacco & Gradese

Venetian · Romance
Monfalcone & Grado areas

Two more Venetian-rooted dialects. Bisiacco is spoken in the Bisiacaria around Monfalcone with a strong Friulian substrate. Gradese belongs to the lagoon town of Grado, historically tied to Venice and Aquileia rather than the Friulian interior.

"Come xétu?""How are you?" (Bisiacco/Gradese)

Plodarisch & Timavese

South Bavarian · Germanic
~1,700 combined · Sappada & Timau

Two more medieval Bavarian "language islands" in the Carnic Alps. Sappada borrows neologisms from German (kòòrn for "truck"), while Timau borrows from Italian (kamion). Each village evolved its own miniature language in isolation.

"Bia geat's dir?""How are you?" (Timavese)

Drive from Bloomington to Indianapolis.
Now imagine crossing 4 languages on the way.

That's the reality of Friuli Venezia Giulia — a region where Romance, Slavic, and Germanic language families collide in less than 3,100 square miles. Three language families, 12+ distinct varieties, one small corner of Italy at the crossroads of Europe.
